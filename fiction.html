A library of one

Best of Fiction.

Fourteen novels I hand people when they ask what to read next. Literary, sci-fi, and the ones I keep going back to.

CompiledApril 2026
Total14 novels
GenresLiterary, Speculative, Contemporary
Genre
  1. № 01

    Lonesome Dove

    By Larry McMurtry

    Eight hundred pages of Gus McCrae and Woodrow Call, two aging Texas Rangers on one last cattle drive from the Rio Grande to Montana. McMurtry set out to dismantle the myth of the American West and accidentally wrote its monument. The friendship at the center is one of the truest things in American fiction. Pulitzer, 1986.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Literary
  2. № 02

    Demon Copperhead

    By Barbara Kingsolver

    Kingsolver takes David Copperfield out of Victorian England and resets it in opioid-era Appalachia. A red-haired kid narrates his own passage through foster care, high-school football, injury, addiction — first person the whole way down, systems failing him at every turn. Earns every inch of the 2023 Pulitzer it shares with Trust.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Literary
  3. № 03

    Stoner

    By John Williams

    The quiet life of a Missouri farm boy who becomes an English professor. Almost nothing happens. Somehow everything does. Forgotten for decades, then rediscovered after a 2003 NYRB reissue — now the standard case for what plain American prose can carry.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Literary
  4. № 04

    East of Eden

    By John Steinbeck

    Two California families retrace the Cain-and-Abel story across three generations in the Salinas Valley. Steinbeck's widest canvas — he thought everything he'd written before was preparation for this. The whole book turns on timshel: the Hebrew word the characters end up reading as "thou mayest." Choice, not fate.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Literary
  5. № 05

    Giovanni's Room

    By James Baldwin

    Paris, 1956. An American engaged to be married starts an affair with an Italian bartender while his fiancée is away. Baldwin's American publisher reportedly told him to burn the manuscript — too gay, too white, too unmarketable. He published it anyway. A first-person confession without self-pity, and one of the foundational texts of American queer literature.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Literary
  6. № 06

    Project Hail Mary

    By Andy Weir

    An astronaut wakes alone in deep space with no memory of who he is or why he's there. He figures it out — and his one job is to save the sun. Weir makes orbital mechanics feel like the plot, and the friendship at the center blindsided me. Lord and Miller's Gosling adaptation hits theaters March 2026.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Speculative
  7. № 07

    Jurassic Park

    By Michael Crichton

    A billionaire clones dinosaurs from amber-preserved DNA and opens a theme park off the coast of Costa Rica. It goes badly. Under the set pieces Crichton is running a real argument about chaos theory, corporate hubris, and the ethics of deploying biotech before anyone understands it. The book is considerably meaner than Spielberg's 1993 film.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Speculative
  8. № 08

    Red Rising

    By Pierce Brown

    On a terraformed Mars run as a color-coded caste system, Darrow is a Red miner pulled into a plot against the ruling Golds. He undergoes reconstructive surgery to pass as one and infiltrates their academy. Seven books in, Brown has built a universe that reads like The Hunger Games if everyone in it grew up on Gibbon. Bloodydamn.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Speculative
  9. № 09

    Dune

    By Frank Herbert

    Ecology, religion, and imperial politics on a desert planet that happens to be the sole source of the spice that makes interstellar travel possible. Herbert invented the playbook that Star Wars, Foundation, and half of modern science fiction have been quoting since. First Nebula winner, Hugo co-winner. Villeneuve's two-part 2021/2024 adaptation finally did it justice.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Speculative
  10. № 10

    Empire of Silence

    By Christopher Ruocchio

    A far-future space opera narrated, memoir-style, by a disgraced nobleman awaiting execution for crimes he's about to explain. Ruocchio writes like Gene Wolfe crossed with Warhammer 40,000 — literary science fiction at operatic scale. Book one of the Sun Eater series. The usual next stop for readers who finish Dune and need somewhere to go.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Speculative
  11. № 11

    It

    By Stephen King

    Seven children in Derry, Maine, confront an ancient evil that takes the shape of their fears — most famously a clown called Pennywise. The book cuts between 1958 and 1985, when the seven return as adults to finish what they started. Over 1,100 pages, and King's best novel about the things small towns hide in plain sight. Not for the squeamish.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Speculative
  12. № 12

    Piranesi

    By Susanna Clarke

    A narrator keeps a daily journal from inside an infinite house of statues, halls, and tides — vast, beautiful, and the whole world he knows. Clarke's first novel in sixteen years, after Jonathan Strange & Mr Norrell. Won the 2021 Women's Prize. Short, strange, and hard to stop thinking about.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Speculative
  13. № 13

    Atmosphere

    By Taylor Jenkins Reid

    NASA, early 1980s. Joan Goodwin is a Rice astronomy professor in the second cohort of female astronauts — training for flight, carrying on a secret relationship with a fellow candidate, and heading toward a Space Shuttle disaster. Reid's latest, set in the same loose universe as Daisy Jones and Evelyn Hugo.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Contemporary
  14. № 14

    The Storied Life of A.J. Fikry

    By Gabrielle Zevin

    A widowed, misanthropic bookshop owner on a fictional New England island finds an abandoned baby in the aisles of his shop. Each chapter opens with the owner's notes on a short story — a device that doubles as a reading list. A word-of-mouth indie-bookstore favorite, over two million copies sold.

    Read at Parnassus Books ↗ Contemporary
